# Editorial Style Guide

## Tone

Write with warmth, clarity, humility, and practical usefulness. Explain rather than prescribe. Avoid mystical certainty, moral judgment, fear, and promises of outcomes.

## Reading level

Use plain language suitable for a broad consumer audience. Define unfamiliar Ayurvedic terms on first use and link to the glossary where helpful.

## Length

- Learning article: TODO words after editorial and usability testing
- Daily post: TODO words; designed for a short mobile reading session
- Headline: concise, specific, and non-alarming

## Sanskrit

Use a Sanskrit term only when it adds meaning. Pair it with a plain-language definition, use an approved spelling consistently, and do not use specialist terminology as decoration.

## Recommendation wording

Prefer optional, bounded language such as “you might try” and explain why a suggestion may be relevant. State uncertainty and offer realistic alternatives. Never imply that following advice guarantees a result.

## Medical language to avoid

Do not diagnose, claim to treat or cure disease, interpret emergencies, recommend medication changes, or present an Ayurvedic assessment as a medical test. Escalate symptom, pregnancy, child, eating-disorder, supplement-interaction, and mental-health content under the approved safety policy.

## Editorial checklist

- Is the intended reader and practical takeaway clear?
- Are Ayurvedic claims traceable to approved expert content?
- Are baseline tendency and recent condition kept distinct?
- Are tags, reviewer, version, and safety notes complete?
- Is AI personalization limited to explicitly permitted elements?
